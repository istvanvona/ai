❯ How did you find the solution?                                                

● Honestly: mostly by elimination, plus one decisive change of viewpoint. Here's
  the actual path, including the dead ends.

  1. Reading the shape of the sequence first. Before testing anything, I
  extracted some structural signals:

  - It's strictly increasing with wildly irregular gaps (+32 here, +295132
  there), so it was either numbers filtered by some property, or a trajectory
  ("each term computed from the previous one").
  - The terms come in odd clusters — 3332/3387, then 6312/6344/6679, then
  25022/25097, 73613/74089, 830520/831194. Tiny steps, then a huge leap.
  - Roughly 6, 10, 13 terms per decade — too sparse for digit-sum-type filters,
  too dense and too irregular for a single smooth formula.

  2. A long graveyard of hypotheses. I tested and discarded a lot:
  factorizations (no shared structure — one term is prime, others are 2×p,
  5³×109...), sums of two squares (3387 ≡ 3·prime kills it), squares+cubes,
  quadratic forms generally (the terms hit every residue class mod 3, 4, 8, 9,
  so no form fits), digit sums, binary/base-7/8/9/12 representations,
  palindromes, Keith numbers, Kaprekar, Carmichael, amicable/sociable numbers
  (those tempting "close pairs" looked amicable-ish!), partition numbers,
  Fibonacci/Lucas/Tribonacci, Cullen/Woodall primes, concatenations of
  squares/primes/Fibonacci numbers, sums of consecutive squares/cubes/primes,
  n·reverse(n) properties... each died on a concrete counterexample, usually
  within the first three terms.

  3. The pivot. After all the "property of n" ideas failed, I went back to the
  difference list — but this time asked a different question: not "what sequence
  do the differences form?" (they form nothing), but "is each difference a 
  function of the term it follows?" That reframing is the whole solution. The
  differences are absurdly non-monotonic (+55, +2925, +32, +335, +6946...),
  which is exactly what digit-dependent functions look like.

  So I stared at 2026 → +1306. First guess: 2026 − 1306 = 720 = 6!, suggesting
  a(n+1) = 2a(n) − 720. It works for one step and fails on the next — classic
  trap. Second guess: digit powers. 2¹ + 0² + 2³ + 6⁴ = 2 + 0 + 8 + 1296 = 1306.
  Hit. Then 3332: 3 + 9 + 27 + 16 = 55. Hit. Then 3387: 3 + 9 + 512 + 2401 =
  2925. Three hits on three different-looking differences is no coincidence —
  and it retroactively explained the clusters: a term ending in 9 takes a giant
  step (9⁵ = 59049), a term full of zeros and small digits crawls (830520 →
  +674). I then verified all 28 transitions by hand, because with a rule this
  cute it's easy to fool yourself with partial checks.

  4. Going backwards. Solving x + f(x) = 2026 is finite work: f is bounded per
  digit count, so I case-split on the digits (thousands digit 1 or 2, then scan
  the hundreds digit, reducing each case to "is d⁴ + d equal to this residue?").
  That exhaustive scan produced exactly two preimages: 1291 (f = 735) and 1760
  (f = 266).

  5. Breaking the tie. The puzzle shows exactly one hidden term, so the hidden
  term should be where the sequence genuinely begins. I ran the preimage search
  again on both candidates: 1760 has a predecessor (1125 + 635 = 1760), so a
  trajectory through 1760 really starts at 1125 and would have two terms before
  2026. 1291 has no predecessor at all — no 1-, 2-, 3- or 4-digit y satisfies y
  + f(y) = 1291 — making it a true seed. Only the trajectory starting at 1291
  puts 2026 in the second slot. Hence 1291.

  If there's a transferable lesson in it: when a sequence resists every
  "intrinsic property" test, switch to treating it as an orbit and regress the
  increments on the previous term — and when increments jump by orders of
  magnitude with no monotonicity, suspect digits, because digits are the only
  "feature" of a number that behaves that discontinuously.
